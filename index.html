What Is the Milky Way?

Our home galaxy — a barred spiral containing everything we have ever known

Structure and Shape

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy about 100,000 light-years wide. It has four main spiral arms and a central bar-shaped bulge of older stars. Our Solar System sits in a smaller arm called the Orion Spur, roughly halfway between the centre and the edge.

The Black Hole at the Centre

At the very centre of the Milky Way sits a supermassive black hole called Sagittarius A*. It has a mass about 4 million times that of our Sun. Despite this, we are safely located 26,000 light-years away from it.

Our Place in the Galaxy

The Sun completes one full orbit of the galactic centre roughly every 225-250 million years, a period sometimes called a cosmic year. In the time since dinosaurs walked the Earth, we have barely moved a fraction of the way around.

More About Our Galaxy

Some of the most fascinating things scientists have discovered

Dark Matter

Around 85% of the Milky Way's total mass is made up of dark matter: a mysterious substance that cannot be seen directly but exerts a gravitational pull. Without it, the outer stars in the galaxy would spin off into space.

How Far Across?

Light travels at 300,000 km per second which is the fastest anything can travel. Even at that speed, it would take 100,000 years to cross the Milky Way from one side to the other. The distances involved are almost impossible to fully picture.

Billions of Worlds

Scientists now believe that almost every star in the Milky Way has at least one planet orbiting it. That means there could be hundreds of billions of worlds in our galaxy alone and we have only explored one of them.
